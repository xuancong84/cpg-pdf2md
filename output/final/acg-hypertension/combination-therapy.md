# Dual Therapy and Combination Prescribing

## Beta Blocker Subclasses

BB subclasses differ in their pharmacodynamic and pharmacokinetic properties due to differences in bioavailability and receptor selectivity (see Table 3).

### Table 3: Prescribing considerations for cardioselective and non-selective beta blockers

| Cardioselective (e.g., atenolol, bisoprolol, metoprolol, nebivolol) | Non-selective (e.g., propranolol, carvedilol) |
|---|---|
| More favourable side effect profile compared to non-selective beta blockers | Preferred for patients who require treatment for migraine prevention, essential tremor, or portal hypertension in cirrhosis (data supports the use of carvedilol also for heart failure) |
| Less likely to cause constriction of airways | |
| Preferred for patients with respiratory diseases, and for management of coronary heart disease, chronic heart failure (bisoprolol, metoprolol, nebivolol), acute coronary syndrome and some arrhythmias | |

The choice of BB should be based on the patient's clinical profile, side effect profile, and dosing frequency (see [Supplementary Table S1](./supplementary-medications.md)).

## Recommendation 4

Consider initiating low-dose dual therapy from two different antihypertensive medication classes based on required BP reduction and cardiovascular risk.
<!-- STRENGTH: conditional -->

> **Note:** "Low-dose" is defined as "initial dose" (see [Supplementary Table S1](./supplementary-medications.md)).

Compared to monotherapy, dual therapy (treatment with two medicines from different antihypertensive classes) is associated with greater magnitude of BP-lowering and better safety profile.

Dual therapy at average to high doses achieves adjusted mean SBP reductions of 20–25 mmHg and DBP reductions of 10–15 mmHg, while monotherapy at average doses achieves adjusted mean SBP reductions of 10–15 mmHg and DBP reduction of 8–10 mmHg.

### When to consider dual therapy

Starting with dual therapy may be appropriate if a greater reduction in BP is required to reach targets:

- SBP/DBP ≥20/10 mmHg above target
- Grade 2 hypertension or higher (clinic BP ≥160/100 mmHg)
- Comorbidities such as DM or CKD requiring more intensive treatment

For elderly or frail patients, the benefits of dual therapy should be weighed against potential harms, such as increased risk of adverse effects, the impact of impaired organ function, or limited tolerability for medications.

### Preferred combinations

While the choice of medication class is largely informed by comorbidities (see [Table 2](./pharmacotherapy-selection.md)) and other patient factors, evidence suggests the most effective combination for reducing cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, and adverse renal outcomes is:

- ACE inhibitor/ARB + dihydropyridine CCB (such as amlodipine), or
- ACE inhibitor/ARB + diuretic (preferably a thiazide-like diuretic)

### Table 4: Antihypertensive combinations to consider avoiding

| Combination | Risks when used in combination |
|---|---|
| ACE inhibitor + ARB | Increased risk of hyperkalaemia, acute kidney injury and lower BP due to similar mechanisms of action |
| BB + non-dihydropyridine CCB (e.g., verapamil) | Increased risk of bradycardia and/or atrioventricular block |
| BB + diuretic | Increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes mellitus |

### Single-pill combinations

Single-pill combinations may be used due to their practical benefits such as reaching BP targets, improved treatment adherence and persistence rates, and lower pill burden. However, single-pill combinations may not be suitable for all patients, such as those who require individualised dose adjustment, or are at risk of adverse effects from medication interactions.

See [Supplementary Table S2](./supplementary-combinations.md) for single-pill combinations registered in Singapore.
