# Management Goals, BP Targets, and Secondary Causes

## Management Goals

The main goals of hypertension management are to achieve significant reduction in BP to attain optimal BP control, delay or prevent progression of hypertension and its associated complications, and mitigate CVD risks. BP levels can be effectively lowered through a tailored management plan, which includes personalised BP targets, sustainable lifestyle intervention, and pharmacotherapy where appropriate.

## Secondary Causes of Hypertension

Secondary hypertension refers to hypertension due to an underlying, identifiable cause. Suspicion of secondary hypertension should be higher in certain circumstances, such as early-onset, severe, or resistant hypertension. A suggestive family history or clinical clues can point to a specific secondary cause, which includes:

- Aortic coarctation
- Certain medications (such as oral contraceptives, NSAIDs, steroids, decongestants, or diet pills)
- Cushing's syndrome
- Excessive alcohol intake (safe/moderate drinking: ≤2 standard drinks/day for men, ≤1 for women; 1 standard drink = 10 g alcohol ≈ 330 mL beer at 5%, 100 mL wine at 15%, or 30 mL spirits at 40%)
- Hyperthyroidism
- Hypothyroidism
- Obstructive sleep apnoea
- Phaeochromocytoma
- Primary aldosteronism (local prevalence ~5% among hypertensive patients)
- Renal parenchymal disease
- Renovascular disease
- Use of illicit drugs

Management of secondary hypertension depends on the underlying cause and can require a combination of lifestyle intervention, cause-specific medications, and surgery. Referral for further investigations and shared management is often required.

## BP Targets

Any reduction in BP decreases the risk of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality.

### Estimated risk reductions due to BP reduction (Figure 1)

Each reduction of **5 mmHg SBP** lowers the risk of major cardiovascular events by ~10% (irrespective of previous diagnosis of CVD, even at normal or high-normal BP values). Major cardiovascular events are defined as a composite of fatal or non-fatal stroke, fatal or non-fatal myocardial infarction or ischaemic heart disease, or heart failure causing death or hospital admission.

A reduction of **10 mmHg SBP** or **5 mmHg DBP** lowers the risk of:

- Heart disease: ~40%
- Stroke: ~35%
- Coronary heart disease: ~15%
- Cardiovascular mortality: ~20%
- All-cause mortality: ~10%

### Guide to setting BP targets (Table 1)

**High to very high cardiovascular risk** (CVD, CKD, DM, HMOD, or SG-FRS-2023 >20%):
- BP target: <130/80 mmHg

**Low to intermediate cardiovascular risk** (none of the above, SG-FRS-2023 ≤20%):
- BP target: <140/90 mmHg
- Consider going lower as tolerated (e.g., <130/80 mmHg)

**Older age** (e.g., >80 years), frail, orthostatic hypotension, or limited life expectancy:
- Consider less stringent BP targets (e.g., <150/90 mmHg)

Individualise BP targets according to patient cardiovascular risk and overall health status; more stringent BP targets can be considered as tolerated by the patient. Do not lower BP below 120/70 mmHg as evidence of benefit beyond this threshold is inconsistent, and the potential for increased risk of side effects can lead to treatment discontinuation.
